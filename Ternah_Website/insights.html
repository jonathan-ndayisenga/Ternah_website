Buy vs. Build:
A Framework for Smarter Decisions

J
Jonathan N.
Software Engineer, Ternah
10 min read

Choosing between off-the-shelf software and a custom build is one of the highest-stakes technology decisions a business makes. Get it right and you move fast, spend efficiently, and stay focused on what makes you competitive. Get it wrong and you spend 3–10x more than expected, delay operations by months, and end up maintaining a system that was never quite right.

This framework cuts through the noise.

First, Define Your Terms

Off-the-shelf software (also called commercial off-the-shelf, or COTS) is pre-built software sold to many customers. Think QuickBooks, Salesforce, SAP, or any SaaS product you pay a subscription for. It is designed to handle common business needs and is ready to deploy in days or weeks.

Custom software is built specifically for your organization, from the ground up, to match your exact processes, data structures, and workflows. It takes months to build, requires ongoing maintenance, and only you own it.

Neither is inherently better. The right answer depends entirely on your context.

The 5-Question Framework

Before writing a single line of code or signing a vendor SLA, answer these five questions honestly.

1. Is this a competitive advantage?

  • Yes (Core differentiator): A unique workflow, proprietary process, or capability that competitors cannot easily replicate. Build.
  • No (Standard business function): Payroll, basic CRM, email, file storage. Buy. Everyone does this the same way, and that is fine.

2. What is your real timeline?

Need something working within three months? Buy. Custom software realistically takes six to eighteen months from discovery to deployment — and that is for a well-run project with clear requirements. If you have external deadlines, compliance dates, or a market window to hit, off-the-shelf is the only sane choice.

3. Can you afford total ownership?

  • Off-the-shelf: Predictable subscription cost. Vendor handles infrastructure, security patches, and feature updates.
  • Custom: Build cost, plus roughly 20% of that cost every year in maintenance, bug fixes, documentation, and onboarding new developers who need to understand the codebase.

A system that costs $50,000 to build costs $10,000/year to maintain indefinitely. Factor this in before you start.

4. Does your team have the expertise to own this?

Custom software without a dedicated product manager and at least one senior architect on the client side almost always fails — not because the code is bad, but because requirements drift, priorities shift, and no one is accountable for the product decisions that need to be made every week.

No internal product leadership? Do not build custom. You will regret it.

5. How stable are your requirements?

  • Changing every quarter: Buy. Off-the-shelf vendors absorb market changes into their product. You benefit without rebuilding.
  • Frozen and well-understood for two or more years: Custom may be worth it. Stable requirements are the single biggest predictor of a successful custom build.

The Golden Rule

Buy commodity. Build differentiators. Integrate everything else.

This is not a compromise. It is a strategy. The best technology organizations in the world buy aggressively for anything that is not their core business, and build only where they can win.

When to Buy

  • Standard business functions: accounting, HR, payroll, file storage, basic project management
  • You are paying for features you do not use — that is fine, because you are also paying for security, compliance, and a team of engineers maintaining it
  • Compliance is the vendor's problem (SOC 2, HIPAA, GDPR) — this alone can justify the subscription cost
  • You are early stage and your processes are still evolving

Rule of thumb: if ten other companies in your industry use the same tool, buy it.

When to Build

  • The software is your product — you are a SaaS company or technology platform
  • Off-the-shelf forces dangerous workarounds: manual re-entry between systems, shadow spreadsheets, staff inventing unofficial processes
  • You have dedicated product leadership and a 12+ month budget
  • Your workflow is genuinely unique — not "we do things slightly differently," but fundamentally incompatible with any existing tool

The Dangerous Middle (Where Money Dies)

Trap 1: "We are special, so we need to build." Most organizations are not as operationally unique as they think. Eighty percent of companies that build custom software could have bought something, configured it well, and moved faster. Challenge your team: have you actually exhausted the market?

Trap 2: "We bought it and now we are hacking it to death." If your team has built more than 20% custom functionality on top of a commercial product, you are now maintaining a custom system anyway — one with the worst of both worlds. Stop. Evaluate whether you should migrate to a proper custom build or a different off-the-shelf product.

Decision Matrix

Your SituationVerdict
Startup, pre-product-market fitBuy everything. Iterate fast.
Scale-up with a truly unique core workflowBuild core. Buy the rest.
Enterprise with 50+ Salesforce customizationsYou built already — just poorly. Evaluate migration.
"No tool can do what we need"90% chance you have not looked hard enough.
Requirements change every quarterBuy. No debate.
Software is your productBuild. That is your job.

The Bottom Line

When in doubt, buy. Most custom software projects fail not because of bad engineering, but because requirements change faster than delivery. The graveyard of failed software projects is full of systems that were beautifully built for a problem that no longer existed by the time they launched.

Save your build budget for what makes you genuinely different. Buy everything else, and buy it fast.

Why Offline-First Matters
in African Markets

J
Jonathan N.
Software Engineer, Ternah
6 min read

Build for the connection you don't have, not the one you might get.

Across Africa, connectivity is real but intermittent. Reliable fiber in Lagos, patchy 3G in Kisumu, no signal at all in rural depots. Apps that assume always-on internet fail. Offline-first software wins.

What "Offline-First" Actually Means

Not "works offline sometimes." Offline-first means the app is designed so that offline is the default state, and online is simply when it syncs.

  • User opens app: works instantly, no spinner
  • Data saved locally first: syncs automatically when connection returns
  • Conflicts resolved automatically: no "error: no network" dead ends

Why It Matters in African Markets

Reality on the GroundOffline-First Advantage
Mobile data is expensiveSyncs once, not hundreds of tiny requests
Power outages reset routersNo lost work — everything cached locally
Users switch networks (WiFi to 4G to 2G)Seamless handoff, no crash
Morning sync window before field workPreload data, then work all day offline

Result: Your app feels 10x faster and more reliable than competitors who assume constant connectivity.

Where Offline-First Wins Most

  • Logistics and delivery: Drivers enter waypoints and delivery updates without needing a signal
  • Healthcare: Community health workers register patients in villages with no coverage
  • Agriculture: Farmers log yields and inputs with no tower for miles
  • Fintech: Agent banking transactions are queued locally and reconciled when connectivity returns
  • Education: Students complete quizzes on a tablet, syncing results when they reach school WiFi

The Technical Reality (Kept Simple)

You do not need a decentralized database. You need four things:

  • Local-first storage: IndexedDB for web apps, SQLite for mobile apps
  • Sync engine: Background, resumable, and conflict-aware
  • Optimistic UI: The interface updates instantly and retries in the background
  • Smart sync policies: Prioritize critical data, defer large media files

Anti-pattern: "We will add offline support later." You will not. Offline-first must be a day-one architectural decision, not a feature added at the end.

The Business Case

  • Higher retention: When something breaks, users blame your app. Offline-first removes the most common failure point.
  • Lower data costs: Apps that batch sync use a fraction of the data of always-online apps, reducing churn in price-sensitive markets.
  • Better trust: "It just works" builds a reputation that spreads by word of mouth in low-connectivity regions.
  • Regulatory edge: Some government and NGO tenders now specifically require offline capability for rural deployment. This is a growing requirement.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Building online-first, then bolting on a "save for later" button at the end
  • Assuming users will patiently wait for a sync spinner to complete
  • Using "last writer wins" conflict resolution — this causes silent data loss
  • Testing only on office WiFi, even with network throttling in developer tools

Offline-First vs. Traditional: A Direct Comparison

FeatureTraditional AppOffline-First App
First loadSpinner, timeout, errorInstant
Weak or no signal"No connection"Works fine
Data usage5 to 10x higherMinimal
User frustrationHighLow

The Bottom Line

Do not design for 100% uptime. Design for 60% uptime and degrade gracefully.

Offline-first is not a technical niche. It is a competitive advantage in African markets. Build it correctly from day one, and you win the users that always-online software consistently leaves behind.

From Manual to Intelligent:
A Practical Roadmap

J
Jonathan N.
Software Engineer, Ternah
8 min read

Spreadsheets and paper are not the enemy. They are survivors. They work. But they do not scale, they hide important insights, and they bleed time. The goal is not to rip them out overnight. It is to migrate without creating chaos.

The Three-Phase Roadmap

Skip the "big bang" ERP disaster. Instead, move through three deliberate phases: digitize, connect, then make it intelligent.

Phase 1: Stabilize and Digitize (Months 0 to 6)

No new processes. No optimization yet. Just replace paper with digital equivalents.

  • Identify your top three pain points: stock cards, attendance registers, invoice approvals
  • Pick one workflow and digitize it exactly as it already works — same fields, same steps
  • Use simple tools: mobile forms, Google Sheets with validation, or a lightweight workflow tool
  • Train one champion per team. Avoid mass training sessions at this stage.

Success metric: Same process, 50% less time, zero manual data re-entry.

Avoid: Automating a broken process. Digitize first, then improve. Putting broken workflows into software just makes them break faster.

Phase 2: Connect and Automate (Months 6 to 12)

Now you can remove redundancy and add intelligent rules.

  • Connect your digitized forms to a central database — stop emailing CSV files between departments
  • Set basic business rules: "Alert when stock falls below 10 units"
  • Automate reminders and approvals: managers get a notification instead of a paper form
  • Introduce one simple dashboard showing today's exceptions — the things that need attention

Success metric: Three workflows fully integrated, manual steps reduced by 70%.

Avoid: Building custom integrations for every edge case. Use tools like Zapier, n8n, or built-in connectors first. Only build custom when you have exhausted existing options.

Phase 3: Intelligent and Predictive (Months 12 to 18)

Now the system starts working for you, not the other way around.

  • Forecast demand based on historical patterns in your own data
  • Flag anomalies automatically: "This month's procurement is twice the usual — review before approving"
  • Surface suggested next actions: "Reorder now to avoid a stockout in five days"
  • Optional: Add document capture via camera for receipts, IDs, and field reports

Success metric: At least one operational decision made by the system without requiring human review.

Avoid: Adding AI for its own sake. Only introduce intelligence where the decisions are repetitive and the underlying data is already clean and structured.

The "No Disruption" Rule

Do ThisNot This
Run old and new systems in parallel for two weeksFlip a switch on a Friday afternoon
Keep spreadsheets available as a backup exportDelete the master spreadsheet on day one
Digitize after the daily rush, such as an end-of-day syncRequire real-time entry during peak operating hours
Let users still print reports if they need toForce tablets onto resistant staff immediately

You are not replacing people. You are replacing friction.

Why This Works for African Organizations Specifically

  • Power and internet gaps: Offline-first mobile forms handle this — see our offline-first guide for details
  • Mixed literacy levels: Use voice input, icons, and local language interfaces wherever possible
  • Low IT budget: Start with free tiers of Airtable, AppSheet, or Trello — they are powerful enough for Phase 1
  • Resistance to change: Find the one person already using a shadow spreadsheet to solve a real problem. That person is your internal champion and your ally.

The Spreadsheet Trap and How to Escape It

Spreadsheets are not evil. They are just fragile once you have more than 50 rows and five users editing simultaneously.

Signs you have outgrown them:

  • "Which version of this file is the latest?"
  • Emailing files back and forth between people
  • Accidental deletions with no audit trail to recover from
  • Copy-paste errors appearing in monthly reports

Escape path: Move the spreadsheet into a database without changing the visual layout. Users see the same columns and rows they are used to, but the data is now in a real system with proper access controls, history, and validation.

Realistic Budget Ranges

PhaseMonthly Cost (USD)Example Tools
Digitize$0 to $200Google Workspace, Google Forms, AppSheet free tier
Connect$200 to $800Airtable, n8n cloud, Microsoft Power Apps
Intelligent$800 to $3,000Retool, Supabase, basic ML APIs

Compare this to the hidden cost of paper: printing, physical storage, lost documents, and an average of two hours per day per person searching for information that should take seconds.

The Bottom Line

Do not try to digitize everything at once. Digitize the three workflows that cause the most firefighting in your organization right now.

Start with procurement approvals, stock reconciliation, or daily field reports. Get those working without disruption. Then expand to the next three. Spreadsheets are not your enemy. Undertrained staff forced into complex software they do not understand is the real problem. Keep it simple. Keep it parallel. Keep it running.

Your first 30-day action item: Pick one paper form your team uses today. Turn it into a mobile form by the end of this week. Run it alongside the paper version for two weeks. No disruption. Just proof that it works.

Turning Dashboards
into Decisions

S
Sheronah A.
IT Consultant, Ternah
7 min read

The difference between reporting that informs and reporting that drives action.

Most dashboards are digital wallpaper. Everyone nods at the charts, then walks away and does the same thing they always did. A good dashboard does not just inform — it demands a next step.

The Three Questions Every Dashboard Must Answer

Before you build a single chart, answer these three questions.

1. Who is looking at this?

  • Executive: one number — "Are we on track?"
  • Manager: exceptions — "What needs my attention?"
  • Operator: actions — "What do I do next?"

2. What decision does this enable?

If the answer is "just awareness," delete the metric. Every number on a dashboard should map to a specific decision: approve, reorder, escalate, or pause. If it cannot trigger a decision, it is noise.

3. When will they act?

  • Real-time data: alert — a red light that cannot be ignored
  • Daily data: a checklist reviewed each morning
  • Weekly data: a trend review for planning

Reporting Dashboard vs. Action Dashboard

FeatureReporting DashboardAction Dashboard
PurposeWhat happenedWhat to do now
Typical contentTrend lines, averagesExceptions, queues, alerts
User response"Hmm, interesting"Opens a task or approval
Update frequencyDaily or weeklyReal-time or hourly
Success metric"Viewed""Clicked through"

The Actionable Dashboard Checklist

  • Each card has a named owner — a person or team who is responsible
  • Each number has a target with red, yellow, and green thresholds
  • Each exception has a suggested action — for example, "Review 5 overdue invoices"
  • Each action is one click away, not behind a separate login
  • No pie charts — they describe, they do not drive action

A Simple Framework: The OODA Loop

Observe, Orient, Decide, Act.

  • Observe: One screen shows today's exceptions — stock below minimum, payments overdue by more than 30 days
  • Orient: Color coding shows severity — red means act now, yellow means plan, green means fine
  • Decide: The dashboard suggests "Reorder 100 units from Supplier X"
  • Act: A button creates the purchase order or assigns the task directly

If your dashboard does not reach the Act step, it is a report, not a tool.

Common Mistakes and Their Fixes

MistakeFix
Too many metrics — analysis paralysisLimit to top 3 Key Performance Indicators (KPIs) per role
Historical data only — yesterday's newsAdd real-time alerts for critical thresholds
Static PDF exported monthlyLive link with filters for each user type
No alerting rules configuredPush notification when stock falls below safety level
Dashboard treated as a trophyDashboard treated as a to-do list

What Action Dashboards Look Like in Practice

  • Procurement: "10 items below reorder point. Click to generate orders."
  • Finance: "5 invoices overdue by more than 30 days. Click to send reminder."
  • Logistics: "3 deliveries delayed by more than 2 hours. Click to reassign driver."
  • Sales: "Leads not contacted in 48 hours. Click to assign to rep."

The Bottom Line

Do not build a dashboard that answers questions. Build one that starts work.

After a user looks at your dashboard, they should know exactly what to do next and be able to do it in two clicks. If they close the tab and nothing changes, you built a report, not a decision tool.

One-page audit: Pick your most-used dashboard. Delete half the charts. Add one action button. Test for a week and measure whether anything changes in response time or task completion.

Automation That
Pays for Itself

S
Sheronah A.
IT Consultant, Ternah
5 min read

Identifying the workflows where automation delivers the fastest, clearest return.

Not every automation makes money. Some just digitize inefficiency. The trick is finding the workflows where the Return on Investment (ROI) is obvious, fast, and hard to argue with.

The Self-Funding Automation Formula

A workflow is a good candidate when it meets this test:

High frequency multiplied by high manual effort, divided by low rule complexity, equals fast payback.

Where to Look First: The Top Five

WorkflowTypical Monthly HoursPayback Period
Data entry (paper to digital)40 to 100 hours1 to 2 months
Approval routing (emails and signatures)30 to 60 hours2 to 3 months
Report generation (copy-paste from multiple sources)20 to 40 hours1 month
Inventory reconciliation (spreadsheet vs. physical count)15 to 30 hours2 months
Customer follow-up (reminders, payment chasing)25 to 50 hours1 to 2 months

The Three Types of ROI — Calculate All of Them

1. Hard ROI (Direct cost savings)

  • Labor hours reduced, multiplied by hourly cost including benefits and overhead
  • Overtime eliminated
  • Late fees or penalties avoided

2. Soft ROI (Productivity gains)

  • Faster turnaround leads to happier customers
  • Staff reallocated to higher-value work
  • Reduced burnout and lower staff turnover

3. Strategic ROI (Risk and compliance)

  • Fewer manual errors such as double payments and misrouted orders
  • Audit trail captured automatically without extra effort
  • Regulatory reporting completed without last-minute panic

The No-Code First Rule

You do not need developers to find your first payback. Start with tools that require no programming:

  • Zapier or Make (formerly Integromat): Connect two apps — for example, Google Forms to Google Sheets to an email alert
  • Power Automate: Best for the Microsoft ecosystem — SharePoint approvals, email parsing
  • n8n: Self-hosted, open-source automation — good when your data is sensitive and cannot leave your servers
  • Retool or Budibase: Internal tools built on top of databases you already have

The 30-Day Automation Sprint

  • Week 1: Map the current workflow in detail — every click, every email, every copy-paste step
  • Week 2: Identify the single most repetitive subtask, not the whole process
  • Week 3: Build a prototype using the free tier of an automation tool
  • Week 4: Run old and new in parallel and measure time saved

Success metric: At least 10 hours per week saved. If yes, roll out. If no, choose a different workflow and repeat.

When Not to Automate

  • The process changes every month — automate stability, not chaos
  • More than 50% of cases require human judgment or exception handling
  • The data source is inconsistent — garbage in, garbage out
  • Compliance requires a manual sign-off at every step regardless

Real Example: African Retail Chain

Before: 12 store managers emailed daily stock sheets. Head office copy-pasted everything into a master spreadsheet. Three hours every morning. Frequent errors from manual entry.

After: Google Form on each manager's phone. Data writes directly to Google Sheets. Automated alert fires if any item falls below reorder point.

Result: 15 hours per week saved at head office. Stockouts reduced by 40%. Full payback in 22 days.

The Bottom Line

Do not automate everything. Automate the boring, the repetitive, and the frequent.

Start with a workflow that annoys someone every single day. If you can save that person one hour daily, that is 250 hours a year — roughly six full work weeks. That pays for itself before the first month is over.

Your Monday morning test: Ask your team, "What task do you dread doing every day?" That is your first automation target.

Building for Scale
from Day One

S
Sheronah A.
IT Consultant, Ternah
9 min read

Architecture choices that save you a painful rebuild as your user base grows.

"We will rebuild it properly when we grow" is a statement that never comes true. You never have time, and by then your data is a mess, your users are frustrated, and your team is fighting fires instead of shipping features. Scaling is not about expensive hardware. It is about smart architectural decisions from the very first line of code.

What "Scale" Actually Means

Scaling is not just about more users. It covers four dimensions that often break independently:

  • Data volume: Moving from 1,000 rows to 10 million rows while keeping queries fast
  • Concurrency: Going from 10 simultaneous users to 10,000 without timeouts
  • Features: Expanding from 5 database tables to 50 without the codebase breaking down
  • Team size: Growing from 1 developer to 10 without them constantly blocking each other

The Five Non-Negotiable Choices

1. Start with a relational database

Use PostgreSQL or MySQL. Avoid flat files, spreadsheets, or JSON blobs as your primary data store. Relational databases give you indexes, joins, and transactions — three capabilities that save you enormous pain as your data grows. NoSQL (Not Only Structured Query Language) databases have their place, but start with SQL (Structured Query Language) until you have a proven reason to move away from it.

2. Keep the application layer stateless

Do not store user sessions on a specific server. Use a shared cache like Redis or store session state in your database. This lets you add more servers behind a load balancer without running into "sticky session" problems where users get logged out randomly.

3. Make operations idempotent

An idempotent operation produces the same result whether it runs once or ten times. No duplicate charges, no duplicate orders. Networks fail and users double-click. Idempotency protects your data integrity automatically.

4. Use asynchronous processing for anything slow

Email sending, report generation, and file processing should go into a queue — tools like RabbitMQ, Amazon SQS (Simple Queue Service), or Bull work well. Your Application Programming Interface (API) returns a fast response, and the slow work happens in the background. Users get a "we will notify you when it is ready" response rather than a spinning wheel.

5. Keep environments identical

Your development, staging, and production environments should match as closely as possible — same database version, same operating system. "It works on my machine" is the single most common cause of scaling failures in small teams.

The "Scale Too Early" Trap

Do not build for Google's scale. Build for ten times your current users. That is always enough headroom for the next 18 months.

Common over-engineering mistakes:

  • Microservices architecture for a team of three developers
  • Kubernetes for an application handling 500 requests per day
  • Database sharding before you have reached 100 gigabytes of data

A single well-structured monolith with a properly indexed database can handle millions of users. WhatsApp ran on PostgreSQL for years at massive scale.

What Breaks First and How to Prevent It

Breaking PointEarly Prevention
Database queries become slowAlways index foreign keys and columns used in WHERE clauses from day one
File uploads fill server storageStore files in cloud storage (Amazon S3 or Cloudflare R2), never in the database itself
Third-party API rate limits hitAdd a queue with retry logic and exponential backoff
Authentication becomes a bottleneckUse token-based authentication (JSON Web Tokens / JWT) from day one rather than server-side session cookies
Logging becomes unreadable chaosUse structured JSON logs with a request ID that traces a single request across all services

The Real Cost of a Scale Rebuild

A "scaling rebuild" at 100,000 users typically costs all of the following at once:

  • Six to twelve months of engineering time that could have built new features
  • A data migration with real risk of corruption or extended downtime
  • Customer churn during the performance problems that triggered the rebuild
  • Opportunity cost of every feature you could not ship during that period

All of this is avoidable with a handful of small decisions made at the beginning.

Simple Checklist for Day One

  • Use a relational database with a proper, normalized schema
  • Keep business logic in application code, not in database stored procedures
  • Make every API endpoint idempotent where possible
  • Send email and process files in background jobs, not in the request cycle
  • Use environment variables for all configuration — no hardcoded URLs or credentials
  • Write database migration scripts from the first commit so schema changes are tracked and reversible

The Bottom Line

Scale is not a feature you add later. It is a set of habits you build from the start.

You do not need Kubernetes or microservices to scale well. You need a clean database schema, stateless application servers, and a queue for slow tasks. That covers 80% of scaling problems. The remaining 20% you will learn when you actually get there — and by then you will have the data and the budget to solve it properly.

Your one-page action item: Review your current architecture against the checklist above. For each item you cannot check off, estimate how many users you can handle before it becomes a crisis. Then decide whether that ceiling is acceptable for the next 18 months.
